1 Triangle Meshes(三角网格)

Triangle meshes are generally used to represent surfaces, so a mesh is not just a collection of unrelated triangles, but rather a network of triangles that connect to one another through shared vertices and edges to form a single continuous surface.

Triangles (B,A,C) and (D,C,A) are consistently oriented, whereas (B,A,C) and (A,C,D) are inconsistently oriented.

1.3 fans and strips

Triangle fan

In an indexed mesh, the triangles array would contain [(0, 1, 2), (0, 2, 3), (0, 3, 4), (0, 4, 5)].

The fan in the ﬁgure could be speciﬁed with the sequence [0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5]: the ﬁrst vertex establishes the center, and subsequently each pair of adjacent vertices (1-2, 2-3, etc.).

Triangle strip

The triangle strip in the ﬁgure could be speciﬁed by the sequence [0 1 2 3 4 5 6 7], and every subsequence of three adjacent vertices (0-1-2, 1-2-3, etc.).

For consistent orientation, every other triangle needs to have its order reversed. In the example, this results in the triangles (0, 1, 2), (2, 1, 3), (2, 3, 4), (4, 3, 5), etc.

1.4.2 winged-edge data structure

makes edges the ﬁrst-class citizen of the data structure.

each edge stores pointers to the two vertices it connects (the head and tail vertices), the two faces it is part of (the left and right faces), and, most importantly, the next and previous edges in the counterclockwise traversal of its left and right faces. Each vertex and face also stores a pointer to a single, arbitrary edge that connects to it.

1.4.3 The Half-Edge Structure

Each half-edge points to the face on its side of the edge and to the vertex at its head, and each contains the edge pointers for its face. It also points to **its neighbor on the other side of the edge.

We no longer need to check orientation, and we follow the pair pointer to access the edges in the opposite face.
